Penske Truck Leasing

Reframing a marketing problem as a service-design problem — uncovering why Penske kept losing new customers during the pandemic-era shift from simple leasing to a complex rent-then-lease model.

Client
Penske Truck Leasing (via Zenda Consulting)
Role
Senior Design Strategist & Project Lead
Year
2022

Challenge

Reduce customer drop-off during onboarding by addressing pandemic-related induced frustration over a two-year delivery wait.

Solution

I went back to the marketing team during the project with evidence that challenged the original idea, shifting the work from a messaging problem to a service redesign. Through 47 interviews across five regions, we found that the service transition from rent-to-lease caused frustration—and created two key tools (the Rhythm of Work model and the Customer Start-up Visual) that gave Penske leaders their first cross-departmental view of the onboarding process from the customer's perspective.

Outcome

Setting the strategic direction for service redesign at specific high-value customer touchpoints. Seven recommendations were adopted, covering workflow management, master data strategy, billing standardization during the rent-to-lease transition, tiered services through customer groups, and a plan to expand field-level innovations.

The problem

As pandemic-driven truck delivery delays mounted, Penske saw more customers slip away. The marketing team assumed frustration over the two-year wait was to blame and focused on messaging. In reality, the pandemic had transformed Penske's service from simple leasing to a complex rent-then-lease model, but the company's structure remained stuck in the pre-pandemic mode. Most customer losses occurred due to friction during the switch between these service models.

Customers weren't leaving because of the two-year wait — they were leaving because the rent-to-lease handoff was a maze the company itself couldn't see.

Stakeholders. Engagement sponsor: Penske's senior marketing leadership. Operational stakeholders: corporate sales operations, customer success, the onboarding team, regional Penske offices, the billing department, and the product team. Customers: businesses across local, national, lease, contract maintenance, and interim-rental-to-lease paths, focusing especially on smaller operators without purchasing staff.

Scale. As one of North America's largest truck leasing companies, Penske's reach is vast. This project spanned five U.S. regions, three distinct customer journeys, and seven departments shaping each new customer's experience. Notably, smaller operators — those most vulnerable — accounted for a significant share of Penske's lost new customers.

Timeline. Approximately 5 months in 2022.

Methods used

Multi-region field interviews

A total of 47 interviews were conducted across five regions — Reading, Rochester, Milwaukee, Sacramento, and Boston — capturing voices from 9 corporate stakeholders, 30 field workers, and 8 customers navigating local mixed-fleet paths. The findings revealed that customers were not frustrated by the two-year wait itself, but by Penske's repeated failure to meet promised delivery dates and confusion caused by the rent-to-lease billing transition.

Ride-alongs with sales reps

Field research included ride-alongs with sales reps, revealing how Lease Sales Representatives patched up broken processes with personal spreadsheets, after-hours calls, and handwritten checklists tucked away in notebooks. These quiet fixes kept the real problems hidden from corporate leaders, as field staff absorbed the fallout. The insight that "every department has its own customer data source" emerged from these observations.

Every department has its own customer data source — an insight that surfaced only after riding along with the people patching the system back together every day.

Billing journey tracing

The team traced the billing journey through both legacy product systems, mapping how customers navigated rent and lease invoices. They pinpointed exactly where confusion set in: invoices changed appearance, details grew harder to decipher, payment methods broke down, and new fuel receipt rules surfaced without warning. The rent-to-lease transition emerged as the main driver of customer losses.

The Rhythm of Work model

The Rhythm of Work model, a strategic diagnostic tool, brought customer groups to life as overlapping waves — each with its own natural work pattern. By mapping Penske's local, customer-led, and corporate-led actions to these rhythms, leaders saw, for the first time, why a one-size-fits-all onboarding process failed some customers. The model also uncovered a hidden group: smaller operators without purchasing staff, who struggled most with Penske's makeshift solutions.

The Customer Start-up Visual

The Customer Start-up Visual became the client's most valued diagnostic tool. This illustration placed the customer at the center, making the maze of scattered contact points visible from their perspective for the first time. It united departments around the customer's true journey, serving as a shared starting point for redesigning the process.

Outcomes

Strategic outcomes

The project pivoted from simply crafting new messaging to uncovering and fixing real issues in the customer onboarding process, raising its value and targeting the true source of customer loss. The team also discovered that low mobile app adoption was rooted in service design flaws, not user experience, steering Penske's product team onto a new path.

Diagnostic outcomes

The investigation revealed the rent-to-lease billing change as the main driver of customer loss. It also brought to light a previously overlooked group — smaller operators without purchasing staff — who were hit hardest by Penske's workarounds. Boston's delivery-delay management system stood out as a local innovation worth expanding.

Deliverable outcomes

Penske adopted a new operations model and seven targeted recommendations, spanning:

  • Workflow management across the rent-to-lease transition.
  • A unified data strategy across departments.
  • Billing standardization for the rent-to-lease transition.
  • Tiered service for different customer types.
  • A roadmap for scaling field-level improvements.
  • A path forward for mobile app adoption rooted in service design.
  • Adoption of the Customer Start-up Visual as the shared cross-departmental reference.
