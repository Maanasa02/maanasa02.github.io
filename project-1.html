Police Hiring Redesign

Redesigning San Francisco's police hiring pipeline to fix a leaky funnel — producing the first net positive year of sworn-officer growth in a decade.

Client
Mayor's Office of Innovation, San Francisco
Role
Design & Project Lead
Year
2025

Challenge

How do you fix a hiring process that loses 70% of candidates during hiring and takes up to two years to produce a sworn officer? Meanwhile, the city has lost a quarter of its force, and overtime costs have gone up by $70 million.

Solution

I looked at the single dropout number as three separate problems — recruitment, onboarding, and academy — each with its own diagnosis and solution, plus a fourth shared effort to build the cross-agency system. Beyond these three parts, we looked at: what kind of officers it should be designed to produce to staff the right priority areas?

Outcome

The time from application to academy dropped from two years to eight months. Background checks went from five months to two. Academy graduation rates increased from 30% to 56%. Lateral applications grew by 364%. Most importantly, the shared hiring dashboard is now managed by SFPD and the Department of Human Resources, ensuring the system's longevity. By October 2025, SFPD ran its largest academy surge in five years, and the city saw its first net increase in sworn officers since the 2020 pandemic.

The problem

In just five years, the number of SFPD officers dropped sharply from 1,900 to 1,400. The city spent an extra $70 million on overtime to cover the shortfall, but the real problem was a broken hiring process: 70% of candidates quit before the academy, and another 70% left during training.

At first, we were brought on to increase the number of applicants so we could hire faster. But after looking closer, we found a leaky pipeline with three main reasons for the dropouts: recruitment, onboarding, and the academy itself. We worked together to create a hiring process with fewer dropouts, resulting in net positive hires (including attrition) for the first time in 10 years.

Changes were made — testing done in one day, twice as many investigators, faster eligibility lists, and a redesigned academy. Results are reviewed after one year, with ongoing improvements: AI tools to handle time-consuming paperwork are growing, and the goal for academy graduation rates is rising from 56% toward a tough 80%.

Our team helped the department focus on what mattered most with the staff they already had — cutting down paperwork and showing a way to have full staffing in key areas, even before hiring new officers.

Stakeholders. Core sponsors: Mayor's Public Safety Chief, SFPD Chief, Department of Human Resources leadership, Mayor's Office of Innovation Director. Core partners: SFPD recruiting team, DHR background-check team, SFPD Data Team, academy training staff.

Timeline. Feb 2025 – Oct 2025.

Methods used

Funnel analysis and segmentation

Eighteen months of applicant data revealed more details. By tracking every step from application to academy graduation, we found that the 70% dropout rate actually covered three separate problems. We also found a hidden chance: only 42 lateral candidates applied in 2024 because no one was contacting them.

Qualitative research with strong candidates who dropped out

Interviews gave us a rare look at the ignored side of the process. They showed the problems: long periods of silence after background checks, confusing evaluation schedules, and no communication during the most stressful waits. The biggest surprise was that candidates were not failing but leaving because of poor communication.

Candidates were not failing — they were leaving, because of poor communication.

Targeted digital marketing tests

Working with SFPD recruiters at career fairs, we saw that usual methods — booths, keychains, generic web ads — did not work well, especially with Gen Z. This change in thinking made us realize recruitment needs a sales approach that really connects with younger people.

Candidate journey mapping

Working with SFPD recruiting and DHR, we tracked every candidate handoff between agencies. This plan showed a major problem: more than half the handoffs had no clear person in charge, no agreement on service, and no shared view of candidate progress. For the first time, leaders could see the system gap causing pipeline failures.

Shared hiring dashboard

I worked with the police department to build a live, shared hiring dashboard using SFPD and DHR data, tracking both work-related and community-impact metrics. This new system is designed to last, owned and managed by SFPD and DHR, so it continues after the Mayor's Office of Innovation leaves.

Actions to improve candidate experience during hiring

Recruitment, onboarding, and the academy each received their own review and tailored solution, moving beyond the idea of a one-size-fits-all fix.

  • Recruitment adopted tailored sales strategies suited for today's audience.
  • Onboarding was sped up by clearly assigning responsibility and sorting strong candidates to help them move faster.
  • The academy was updated to meet the needs of today's recruits.

Demand and capacity analysis

We studied demand and capacity, matching call numbers and problem areas with officer availability. The analysis showed that shortages affected the neighborhoods and shifts most important to the Mayor's priorities. Even with more applications, the 10-person investigator team was the real bottleneck. This started a wider discussion among city leaders about what the hiring process should focus on first.

  • Lateral applications +364%.

  • Application-to-academy 2 years → 8 months.
  • Academy graduation 30% → 56%.

System outcomes

  • First net positive growth in sworn officers since the 2020 pandemic.
  • Four academy classes in 2025 with a fifth scheduled for December — the largest academy surge in five years.
  • Citywide crime down 30% to its lowest level in decades by October 2025.

Strategic outcomes

  • Reframed the question from "hire faster" to "what should the pipeline be designed to produce" — now an active conversation in city leadership.
  • Cross-agency dashboard owned by SFPD and DHR (not MOI), so capability survives MOI's departure.

Capability outcomes

  • HR–SFPD pipeline operating with named owners for every handoff and shared metrics across both agencies.
  • Service blueprint adopted as the operational reference for ongoing pipeline refinement.
