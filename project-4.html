End-to-end eCommerce Product Labeling Operations

Mapping the content operations behind Johnson & Johnson's ecommerce product labeling and surfacing structural gaps blocking their Digital First transformation.

Client
Johnson & Johnson (via Zenda Consulting)
Role
Senior Design Strategist
Year
2023

Challenge

J&J's Global Labeling Operations team lacked a clear view of how product content moved from creation to the final product page. Leadership wanted to go Digital-First, but they could not see the entire system.

Solution

We created a Data Operations model that made the whole content lifecycle easy to understand. It highlighted gaps between teams and helped leadership decide which issues to fix first.

Outcome

Leadership used a shared resource to spot communication gaps and decide where to invest. The focus changed from just documenting the process to figuring out where and when to invest.

My role

Senior Design Strategist on a three-person team with a project lead and a researcher. I led the interviews with directors, managers, and team leads, drove the cross-team process mapping, and structured the operations into the Data Operations model that became the engagement's primary deliverable.

The problem

User problem: Consumers on J&J product pages faced inconsistencies and slow updates. Content moved through disconnected systems via manual re-entry, and the teams making early content decisions could not see how consumers actually responded to the content they shipped.

Business problem: Leadership wanted to drive a Digital First transformation but could not see how content operations worked end-to-end. Local workarounds masked structural gaps, ownership of the data lifecycle was scattered, and there was no shared roadmap for where to invest first.

J&J aimed to focus on digital, putting online consumer engagement and visibility first. Product content went through many people and systems before it appeared on a product page. No one in Global Labeling Operations had a clear view of this process. Gaps between teams were hidden, with individuals covering them through manual work.

Leadership was expected to drive a Digital First transformation, but they could not see how the system worked. Existing issues within the current state of operations weren't obvious because teams had created local workarounds.

Methods used

In-depth interviews with directors, managers, and team leads

People were open about their frustrations. The problems were clear, but no one had brought them together in one place.

Collaborative process mapping

We worked with stakeholders to map out how content moved from creation to modification, storage, and digital execution. This made it clear where teams were disconnected.

Structuring the Data Operations model

I led the process of organizing operations into a Data Operations model with four main areas around a central Digital Content Ecosystem: Upstream (gathering label data), Performance (understanding and learning), Delivery (label delivery), and Downstream (label curation). We measured each gap by the amount of manual work required, the number of teams affected, and the extent of the impact. This helped turn the model into a roadmap for leadership to set priorities.

The interviews showed us the pain points. The map revealed how these issues were connected. The weighting helped leadership know where to begin.

The methods above sit within two evaluation approaches. Process evaluation through in-depth interviews and collaborative process mapping with directors, managers, and team leads surfaced where content operations were breaking. The Data Operations model functions as a theory of change, laying out how restructuring around four areas (Upstream, Performance, Delivery, Downstream) would produce a working content lifecycle. The impact-weighted gap analysis applies the Four Es framework (economy, efficiency, effectiveness, equity) to give leadership a basis for sequencing investment.

What surfaced

Post-performance data was not used enough

The company collected information on how products performed after reaching consumers, but not everyone could access it. Teams working on digital execution could see the data, but those making early content decisions often could not.

The organization's structure made it hard to see the full data lifecycle

Ownership was scattered, and handoffs between groups lacked a shared understanding. New staff learned by following the most experienced people. The system relied on informal knowledge.

Disconnected systems meant teams had to use manual workarounds

Different teams owned different software, and these systems did not connect. Content updates often had to be sent back two or three times before going live, and teams no longer questioned this extra work.

System-wide issues made it hard to focus on consumers

Any team that tried to improve things on its own ran into the same problems.

Teams creating product content could not see how consumers reacted to it. Teams who saw the consumer response could not influence what was created.

Interventions

We recommended four changes for leadership, each linked to a key finding and prioritized for order of action.

Build smarter data sets that reflect consumer sentiment

So content is shaped by how consumers engage with it.

Establish data standards for shared understanding

So that the same product information means the same thing across systems.

Restructure teams for cross-functionality and clearer ownership

So the data lifecycle has owners at each stage.

Streamline data flows by reducing tech complexity

So staff stop absorbing the cost of disconnected systems.

The final presentation explained each recommended change, the specific gaps it addressed, the teams involved, and the suggested order based on impact and feasibility.

We produced a sequenced list of recommendations, showing what to do first so the next step and how to prioritize them.

Outcome

The Data Operations model became a shared tool for leadership to spot communication gaps and opportunities across the content lifecycle. For the first time, directors and managers could see a single view of how content moved through the organization and where they could make the biggest impact.

The deliverable changed the conversation in Global Labeling Operations from just documenting the process to deciding where and when to invest. It laid the groundwork for the Digital First strategy the organization wanted to build.

Context

Stakeholders. J&J Global Labeling Operations leadership, directors, managers, and team leads.
