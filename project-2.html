San Francisco's Homeless Response

A cross-departmental design effort to reshape how San Francisco's homeless response system serves its most vulnerable residents.

Client
Mayor's Office of Innovation, San Francisco
Role
Design Lead
Year
2024–2025

Challenge

How do you coordinate care for people experiencing homelessness when seven city departments work with the same person, none of them can see what the others are doing, and the most vulnerable clients — 9% of the unhoused population using 50% of all services — keep cycling through emergency services without ever moving toward stable care?

Solution

I changed the focus from a data integration problem to redesigning accountability and led the work in four parts — a shared plan for change across departments, a reusable legal framework for sharing data, combined data based on a Shared Priority List, and a field tool we eventually stopped using when the basic parts worked well enough on their own.

Outcome

The operational backbone of citywide reform: A four-month prototype showed promising results for two-thirds of San Francisco's 15 most vulnerable clients getting into housing, shelter, treatment, or case management — with no deaths and no overdoses. ASTRID is now the main system behind Mayor Lurie's Neighborhood Street Teams model, expanding across more than 7 departments.

The problem

Seven city departments — ranging from Public Health to Human Services — all worked with people experiencing homelessness, yet each operated in isolation. There was no shared definition of a client, and no one took full responsibility for guiding individuals from first contact through the several eligibility checks and resource matches to care or housing. While the brief described this as a data integration issue, the real challenge was the absence of a unified, cross-departmental view of the client, their needs and resource matches.

Stakeholders spanned every level: from the Mayor's Office of Innovation and department chiefs, to outreach workers and paramedics on the front lines. Core partners included Public Health, Fire, Emergency Management, and Homelessness and Supportive Housing, later joined by HSA, Police, Public Works, and the Medical Examiner in ASTRID 2.0. Funding came from Bloomberg Philanthropies and Johns Hopkins University. Ultimately, the work focused on serving about 16,000 unhoused San Franciscans, with special focus on the 191 people that we identified as the most vulnerable individuals.

Scale was ambitious: the pilot integrated 125,000 street encounters across four departments, with ASTRID 2.0 expanding to seven departments and over 120 users.

Timeline. 2023 – Present.

Methods used

Mixed Method research

We used a mix of field research methods, engaging leaders and frontline staff from DPH, Fire, DEM, and HSH. Through interviews, ride-alongs, and direct observation of care coordination meetings, we uncovered that the real breakdowns happened during handoffs between departments — not within them. This insight became the foundation for reframing the problem.

Theory of Change Workshops

We brought leaders from DPH, Fire, DEM, and HSH together for six hands-on workshops, each grounded in real client stories. These sessions mapped out operations, established clear warm-handoff protocols, and assigned ownership for transitions that had previously been neglected. The result was San Francisco's first unified, cross-departmental goal for street response — a shared vision. These were tough stakeholders that needed to really buy into the idea of taking on more work than the regulation demanded of them.

Service Blueprint

Together with department directors, we created a service blueprint that traced the client journey across four departments. By highlighting where handoffs lacked ownership, the blueprint became a powerful tool — making the coordination problem tangible for city leaders and inspiring their commitment to redesign.

Legal framework design through the city's Health Master Data Trust

Partnered with the City Attorney's office and department legal teams to establish reusable legal scaffolding for cross-departmental data sharing under HIPAA and 42 CFR Part 2. Produced the legal authority that made integrated data lawful and made the framework reusable for subsequent MOI projects rather than single-use.

"Hackathon/reviewathon" as an introductory method to get staff to use the data

Designed and led a two-week working session in September 2024, it brought data stewards from across San Francisco into the same room to iterate on the product. Produced not just a working integrated dataset, but stakeholder ownership across the city's data community; collaboration patterns continue to inform subsequent MOI engagements.

The Shared Priority List

The Shared Priority List was crafted to be more than just a data tool — it was designed to iterate on creating clear hand-offs and be actionable. It allowed four departments to agree on which clients needed coordinated care, sidestepping debates over whose clients mattered most. Structured care-planning fields were co-developed through observing real coordination meetings, resulting in an operational tool now central to the Lurie Administration's Neighborhood Street Teams.

Four-month pilot

We launched a focused, four-month pilot with 15 clients to test whether all four layers of the intervention worked in practice. By tracking client outcomes and gathering feedback from field staff, we built a compelling evidence base: 2/3 of clients moved into housing, shelter, treatment, or case management, with no deaths or overdoses.

Outcomes

Client outcomes

Pilot, 15 most-vulnerable clients:

  • 2/3 moved to housing, shelter, treatment, or case management.
  • 0 deaths, 0 overdoses.
  • 50% reduction in hospital transports.

System outcomes

  • First cross-departmental by-name list in San Francisco history (125,000 encounters, ~16,000 unique individuals across 4 departments).
  • Operational cost reduced from $1,500/mo to $100/mo per client.
  • Run time from 1 hour to 10 minutes.
  • Daily updates instead of weekly.

Strategic outcomes

  • ASTRID became the operational backbone of the Lurie Administration's Neighborhood Street Teams model.
  • Cited as foundational in Mayor Lurie's March 2025 Executive Directive on homelessness.
  • Selected for a 3-year, $7M Bloomberg Philanthropies partnership — the largest grant Bloomberg has awarded to a city innovation office.

Capability outcomes

  • HMDT legal framework, theory-of-change methodology, and "hackathon/reviewathon" pattern now reusable infrastructure for subsequent MOI engagements.
  • ASTRID 2.0 scaling to 7+ departments and 120+ users.
